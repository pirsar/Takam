Nestled in the lush heights of Mazandaran, where two rivers meet, the village of Takam still breathes. Unlike many rural areas across Iran that have faded away under the waves of migration, Takam remains alive. Agriculture is still practiced; the gardens and fields are still green. Though many of its people now live in nearby cities like Sari during the week, they return every weekend to embrace their ancestral land.

This semi-stable dynamic has transformed Takam into a second-home settlement, a space between memory and hope. Yet behind this seemingly vibrant façade, slow-burning threats are emerging — threats that may soon jeopardize the natural and social fabric of the village.

Threat #1: Deforestation

Mazandaran, once densely forested, is now witnessing one of the most rapid and extensive phases of deforestation in its history. Unregulated logging, land-use change, illegal construction, and timber smuggling are reducing its forest cover at an alarming rate. Though Takam is still located in a relatively pristine and wooded area, it is not immune. If the surrounding forests disappear, the village may suffer declining local rainfall, severe soil erosion, landslides, and the collapse of the ecosystems that support its agriculture.

Threat #2: Water Transfer Projects

In recent years, plans have emerged to transfer water from Mazandaran to drier provinces such as Semnan. While these projects are often justified by the need to supply drinking water, their impact on the source regions is potentially devastating. The rivers that breathe life into villages like Takam may dry up or lose significant flow. Agriculture, forests, and even underground water reserves could be severely harmed.

Threat #3: Unregulated Second-Home Construction

Like many picturesque villages in Mazandaran, Takam has seen a rise in second-home building over the past few decades. While the weekend influx of urban dwellers can bring short-term economic activity, the gradual transformation of the village into an identity-less recreational zone brings deep-rooted harm. This trend often leads to the loss of farmland, environmental degradation, and the unraveling of traditional community bonds.

Conclusion: An Uncertain Tomorrow

Takam lives today, but its future is far from guaranteed. If current trends — deforestation, water diversion, and uncontrolled development — continue, this village could one day be added to the growing list of abandoned or damaged rural landscapes. Erosion, in this sense, does not begin with a landslide, but with silence — slow, quiet, but relentless.

Steps to Protect Takam

  • Establish grassroots environmental and cultural organizations within the village
  • Oppose destructive water-transfer projects through legal and civic means
  • Support local farming and prevent farmland-to-construction conversion
  • Promote sustainable architecture rooted in traditional styles
  • Document and preserve Takam’s cultural, historical, and ecological identity

Takam, with all its beauty and resilience, now needs care and commitment. Let us not be mere observers — but become defenders of its survival.
