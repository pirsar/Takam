Ezdar: The Sacred Tree

A comparative study of the cultural and religious significance of the Ezdar tree in Iran, the Caucasus, Japan, and Korea

Introduction

In many cultures, certain trees are not merely part of the landscape but carriers of meaning, stories, and sanctity. The tree Zelkova carpinifolia, locally known in northern Iran as Ezdar, is one such example. This tree, growing in the Hyrcanian moist forests and the Caucasus region, holds an important place not only ecologically but also in the cultural and spiritual traditions of several peoples. Its sister species, Zelkova serrata, has a similar revered status in East Asia, notably Japan and Korea. This article presents a comparative study of the cultural, religious, and environmental roles of this tree across four world regions.

Scientific and Biological Overview

Scientific name: Zelkova carpinifolia
Family: Ulmaceae (Elm family)
Habitat: Northern Iran's forests, Caucasus, Northern Turkey

Physical Characteristics

  • Height: 20–30 meters
  • Leaves: Oval, serrated, simple, alternate arrangement
  • Bark: Smooth and gray in youth, becoming deeply fissured and cracked with age
  • Wood: Extremely hard, resistant to moisture and insects, light to dark brown

Ecological Role

  • Grows naturally in moist, well-drained mountainous soils
  • Found at altitudes of 400 to 1200 meters above sea level
  • Important in preventing soil erosion, maintaining biodiversity, and carbon storage

Traditional Uses

  • Wood used in making heavy doors, agricultural tools, furniture, and religious objects
  • Valued in rural areas for its hardness and durability

Conservation Status

  • Populations declining due to climate change, deforestation, and overharvesting
  • Listed as vulnerable in some regional conservation assessments

Cultural and Religious Significance

Iran — Ezdar, the Guardian of Imamzadehs

In northern provinces of Iran such as Mazandaran, Gilan, and Golestan, the Ezdar tree is a familiar and respected presence. Its name evokes the toughness of its wood and the sturdiness of its stature. Beyond its physical qualities, it holds spiritual and cultural significance among local people:

  • Commonly found beside Imamzadehs (holy shrines), old cemeteries, and mosques
  • Believed that cutting these sacred trees may bring misfortune or illness
  • Wood often used to craft shrine doors or sacred objects
  • Sometimes regarded as the abode of benevolent spirits or as protection against the evil eye

For villagers, Ezdar is not merely a tree but a "silent witness" of faith and history.

Caucasus — Church Trees

In Georgia and Azerbaijan, Ezdar grows in mountainous, humid valleys. Like in Iran, it is often found near old Orthodox churches, monasteries, or hermitages:

  • Local people tie prayer threads or sacred stones around old trees
  • Believed to protect villages and people from calamities
  • Stories warn of divine wrath if a Zelkova tree is cut

Japan — The Shinto Divine Tree

In Japan, Zelkova serrata (called Keyaki) is considered sacred:

  • Planted at entrances of many Shinto shrines and called shinboku (divine tree)
  • Surrounded by a sacred rope called shimenawa that marks the presence of a spirit (kami)
  • Used as a place of prayer and worship
  • Old Keyaki trees symbolize endurance and communal spirit in villages

Korea — The Village Guardian Tree

In South Korea, Zelkova serrata (called Neutinamu) is planted in village centers and considered a "guardian tree":

  • A gathering place for community discussions and religious ceremonies
  • Prayers are offered beneath it during droughts or natural disasters
  • Often decorated with statues of spiritual guardians or prayer inscriptions

Conclusion

The Ezdar tree, across its various species and regions, is far more than a natural element; it forms a bridge between humans, nature, and spirituality. Whether beside Iran’s Imamzadehs, the Caucasus churches, Japan’s Shinto shrines, or Korean village squares, the tree reflects a universal symbolic role.

These cultures, without direct contact, honor Ezdar or Keyaki as a guardian, healer, and symbol of divine power. This cultural parallelism reveals humanity’s deep-rooted belief in venerable trees as carriers of wisdom and protectors of life.

Protection Suggestions

  1. Identification and Registration of Local Natural Heritage: Ancient Ezdar trees should be officially recognized and registered as spiritual and biological heritage to protect them from destruction.
  2. Targeted Reforestation Efforts: Restoration projects should include replanting Zelkova species, especially in moist mountainous regions.
  3. Documentation and Publication of Local Traditions: Oral stories and beliefs about Ezdar should be collected, documented, and published in books or digital media to preserve cultural knowledge.
  4. Legal Protection: Zelkova species should be listed as vulnerable or protected under national and regional natural resource laws, with restrictions on indiscriminate cutting.
  5. Intercultural Dialogue: Holding conferences, exhibitions, and joint programs among countries that honor Ezdar (Iran, Japan, Korea, Caucasus) can foster shared spiritual understanding and conservation cooperation.

Final Words

Ezdar stands as a living memory; a sign of endurance, a connection between earth and sky, rooted deeply in the ground with branches reaching toward the light.

Protecting this tree means more than conserving a species — it is safeguarding culture, faith, and our collective memory; a bridge between nature and spirituality that resonates through history and belief.

Let us all commit to preserving this sacred and living symbol, so future generations may benefit from its blessings and wisdom.
